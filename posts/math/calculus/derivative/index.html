The Derivative

Slope

Let’s plot $y(x) = x$

Awesome.

That done, let’s figure out what the slope of that function is. This provides us with a measure of how steep the line is. Put another way, we’re asking, “how quickly is $y$ changing when $x$ changes?”

We see that we can answer that question by noting that every time we increase $x$ by one unit, $y$ also increases by one unit.

This suggests that a natural way to define the slope $s$ is as the rise (i.e., how much $y$ increases when $x$ increases by one unit) over the run (i.e., an increase in $x$ by one unit)

$$ s = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} $$

where $\Delta$ is the Greek letter capital delta and is generally used in math and physics to mean “change in”. Applying this to our line, we see

$$ s = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_\mathrm{final} - y_\mathrm{init}}{x_\mathrm{final}-x_\mathrm{init}} = \frac{y(x=2) - y(x=1)}{2 - 1} = \frac{2 - 1}{2 - 1} = 1 $$

So, the line has a slope, or steepness, of one.

Wonderful.

Slope of a Curve

Ok, finding the slope of a line is all well and good, but what about functions that have curves in them, e.g., $y(x) = x^2$?

For a function with curves in it, we see that the slope is different at every point! This notion seems to imply that what we need is, in fact, another function; one whose input is the point on the original curve that we want to know the steepness for and whose output is the steepness of the curve at that point. Of course, it’s all well and good to say things like this, but, how to actually figure out what that function is?

Well, for starters, let’s look at what we did for finding the slope of a line: we calculated $\Delta y$, then $\Delta x$, and then divided those two quantities (rise divided by run). Ok. For a line, it didn’t matter what $\Delta x$ was (with the understanding that $\Delta y = y(x_\mathrm{final}) - y(x_\mathrm{initial})$). That is, our range between $x_\mathrm{final}$ and $x_\mathrm{initial}$ could be as large or as small as we wanted because the steepness of the line was the same everywhere. Here, however, as noted above, the steepness is constantly changing.

This seems to suggest that, in order to determine how steep the function is at a particular point, we need to include that point in our $\Delta x$. But where should we put the endpoints of the range? That is, how wide should $\Delta x$ be?
